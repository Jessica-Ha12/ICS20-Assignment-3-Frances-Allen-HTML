Frances Elizabeth Allen

August 4, 1932 - August 4, 2020

Time-Line

1932

Frances Allen is born on august 4, 1932 in Peru, New York, US. She grew up on a farm and attended a local high school. She is the oldest of six children.

1954

Received her Bachelors Degree in Mathematics from Albany State Teachers College.

1957

Received he Masters Degree in Mathematics as well from the University of Michigan.
She was deeply in debt with student loans, and joined IBM Research as a programmer in 1957.She taught incoming employees the basics of Fortran. She planned to return to teaching once her loans had been paid, but ended up staying in IBM for her 45 year career.

1959

In 1959, Allen was assigned to the Harvest project for code breaking with the National Security Agency. She also worked on a programming language called Alpha.

1962

She managed a compiler-optimization team for Harvest and Stretch project.

1970s

In the 1970s, she Worked with fellow researcher John Cocke to write a series of seminal papers on optimizing compilers, helping to improve the efficiency of machine code translated from high-level languages.
From 1970-1971, Allen took a break from work at New York University, and acted as adjunct professor for a few years.

1972

In 1972, Frances Allen married a New York University professor Jacob T. Schwartz, but later divorced in 1982.

1980-1995

Allen led IBM's work in the developing parallel computing area and helped develop software for the IBM Blue Gene project.

2002

Allen retired from IBM in 2002, but still remained active in programs that encourage women and girls to seek careers in science and computing.

Accomplishements

1994

Nominated as a Fellow of the American Academy of Arts and Science.

1997

Received IEEE Computer Society Charles Babbage Award, inducted into the Witi Hall of Fame

2000

Fellow of the Computer History Museum

2001

Elected to the American Philosophical Society

2002

Augusta Ada Lovelace Award from the Association for Women in Computing.

2004

Winner of the ABIE Award for Technical Leadership
Computer Pioneer Award of the IEEE Computer Society

2006

First woman to win A.M. Turing Award.

2007

The IBM PHD Fellowship Award was created in her honor.

2009

Awarded an honorary doctor of science degree from McGill University for “pioneering contributions to the theory and practice of optimizing computer technique that laid the foundation for modern optimizing and automatic parallel execution”

2010

Elected to the National Academy of sciences.


August 4, 2020

Frances Allen died from complications with Alzeimer’s disease on her birthday.
